Design Brief

The Problem

Elderly and chronically ill patients frequently miss doses or accidentally double-dose because standard pill bottles give no indication of whether today's dose has already been taken.

Existing pill organizers are bulky and don't attach to the original bottle. Smart electronic reminders require charging and are often too complex for elderly users. Seemingly great options are very expensive.

Our Solution

A passive mechanical cap that automatically advances a visible day indicator each time the bottle is opened—no batteries, no apps, no extra equipment.

Design Requirements

  • MUSTDisplay the current day of the week clearly
  • MUSTAdvance automatically when the cap is opened
  • MUSTFit a standard 28 mm pill bottle thread
  • MUSTRequire no batteries or electronics
  • SHOULDBe printable on a consumer FDM 3D printer
  • SHOULDBe child-resistant
  • COULDInclude a "missed dose" visual indicator
  • COULDAccommodate a weekly reset by the user

👥

User Interviews

We interviewed 25 potential users including elderly patients and caregivers. Key insight: users want something that works without thinking—zero cognitive load.

🔍

Existing Products

Analysed e-Pill TimeCap, Odaro Pill Reminder, and Colorwing Smart Pill Dispenser. Found a gap: nothing uses a passive mechanical advance mechanism on the cap itself.

⚙️

Mechanical Research

Researched gear train systems, cam follower mechanisms, and snap-fit joints. These directly informed our final gear-driven mechanism.

📐

Materials Research

Investigated PLA, ABS, and PETG. Selected PLA for prototyping (easy to print, food-safe options available), steel for springs.

04

Design Process

I

Concept Sketches

We generated 2 initial concept sketches exploring different mechanical approaches.

II

Design Development

From our concepts, we selected 1 promising direction and developed detailed annotated drawings specifying key dimensions and mechanisms.

III

CAD Modelling

We modelled all 3 components in SolidWorks. The assembly includes the bottle base, indicator disk, and cap casing.

See the interactive 3D models in the next section.

IV

Prototype & Testing

We 3D printed a first prototype in PLA. Testing revealed a faulty cap design, which led us to remodel the structure of the cap. A second prototype confirmed the fix.

V

Final Design

After 4 rounds of iteration and feedback, we finalized the design. The final cap uses a 7-tooth drive gear meshing with the indicator disk, advancing one day position per opening twist.

Feedback & Evaluation

User Feedback

"This is so much simpler than the pill organizer I currently use. I can check at a glance whether I've taken my tablet today."

— Interview Participant, age 68

"Turning the cap and watching the gear move the day forward is really intuitive — you can see exactly how it works just by looking at it."

— Prototype Testing Participant

"I'd want the window to be a bit bigger so I can read it without my glasses."

— User Feedback, Round 2

How We Responded

FeedbackChange Made
Window too small to readRedesigned window to a trapezoidal aperture 0.25 in. tall with sides angled 51º apart
Disk slipped on releaseAdded a detent feature to the gear teeth to hold position
Thread too hard to gripAdded knurled grip to outer cap
