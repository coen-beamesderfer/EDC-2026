02

Design Brief

I

Client

Central York High School Engineering and Design Competition—a program that challenges students to identify a real-world problem and develop a physical design solution that demonstrates the full engineering design process from research through prototyping.

II

Target Consumer

Elderly adults and chronically ill patients who take one or more daily oral medications and who may have difficulty remembering whether a dose has already been taken. This includes individuals who manage their own medications independently as well as those assisted by a caregiver.

III

Designer

Coen Beamesderfer is responsible for research, user interviews, and all written documentation. Abigail Zayatz is the CAD lead, responsible for translating the concept sketches and detailed design drawings into accurate 3D models in SolidWorks and producing the final files used for prototyping.

IV

Problem Statement

Many elderly and chronically ill patients miss doses or accidentally take a double dose because standard pill bottles provide no record of whether the day's medication has already been taken. Existing solutions—weekly pill organizers, electronic reminder devices, and smartphone apps—are either too bulky, too expensive, require charging, or demand a level of technological familiarity that many older users do not have. There is currently no simple, battery-free, low-cost device that attaches directly to a standard pill bottle and tells a patient at a glance whether they have taken today's medication.

V

Design Statement

We will design a replacement pill-bottle cap consisting of two main components: a rotating indicator disk labeled with the days of the week, and a cap casing fitted with internal slots that hold the disk securely in each day position. After taking their medication, the user manually slides the disk forward to the current day; a window cut into the side of the casing always displays the selected day. The cap will thread onto a standard 28 mm pharmaceutical bottle, require no batteries or electronics, and be fully producible on a consumer FDM 3D printer—making it affordable, accessible, and straightforward for the target consumer to use.

VI

Criteria

  • MUSTDisplay the current day of the week through a window in the cap casing
  • MUSTAllow the user to manually advance the indicator disk by sliding it
  • MUSTThread onto a standard 28 mm pharmaceutical bottle neck
  • MUSTRequire no batteries or electronics of any kind
  • SHOULDUse internal slots to hold the disk firmly at each day position
  • SHOULDBe fully printable on a consumer FDM 3D printer
  • COULDInclude a reset feature to return the disk to the start of the weekly cycle
  • COULDIncorporate a child-resistant opening mechanism
VII

Constraints

  • MATERIALAll printed components must use PLA filament
  • SIZEOuter diameter must not significantly exceed that of a standard pill bottle cap
  • COSTPrototype production must remain within the competition budget allowance
  • TIMEAll design work and documentation must be completed within the competition timeline
  • SOFTWAREAll CAD modeling must be completed in SolidWorks
  • SAFETYAll components must be non-toxic and safe for handling by elderly users

👥

User Interviews

We interviewed 25 potential users including elderly patients and caregivers. The key finding was consistent: users want something that works without thinking—zero cognitive load and no setup required.

🔍

Existing Products

We analysed the e-Pill TimeCap, Odaro Pill Reminder, and Colorwing Smart Pill Dispenser. A clear gap emerged: no existing product offers a simple, manual, battery-free day indicator built directly into the cap itself.

⚙️

Mechanical Research

We researched snap-fit joints, detent mechanisms, and slotted track systems. A slotted-disk approach—where the indicator disk seats into discrete day positions inside the casing—proved the most reliable and manufacturable option within our constraints.

📐

Materials Research

We investigated PLA, ABS, and PETG. PLA was selected for prototyping due to its ease of printing, dimensional accuracy on consumer FDM printers, and the availability of food-safe grades.
